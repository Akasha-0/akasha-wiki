# Nicholas Glynos

Nicholas G. Glynos is a researcher whose work has significantly advanced our understanding of DMT biosynthesis and neurophysiological mechanisms.

## Key Publications

### INMT is Not Essential for DMT Production (2023)
**Glynos NG et al. (2023).** "Indolethylamine N-methyltransferase (INMT) is not essential for endogenous tryptamine-dependent methylation activity in rats." *Scientific Reports* 13:280.

This critical study demonstrated that INMT (traditionally considered the enzyme responsible for DMT synthesis) is **not essential** for DMT production in rats. This finding suggests there are **alternative enzymatic pathways** for endogenous DMT biosynthesis, challenging the long-held assumption that INMT is the primary enzyme involved.

### DMT Neurophysiology (2024)
**Glynos NG et al. (2024).** "Neurochemical and neurophysiological effects of intravenous administration of N,N-dimethyltryptamine in rats." *bioRxiv*.

This study found that intravenous DMT administration in rats:
- Increased serotonin and dopamine levels
- Altered EEG spectral power
- Enhanced functional connectivity
- **Detected endogenous DMT in prefrontal and somatosensory cortices at levels comparable to serotonin and dopamine**

This suggests that endogenous DMT may have **functional significance in the mammalian brain** and that DMT may accumulate and be stored in neuron vesicles.

## Significance

These findings are important because they:
1. Reveal **alternative biosynthesis pathways** for DMT beyond INMT
2. Provide evidence for **functional roles** of endogenous DMT in brain physiology
3. Support the hypothesis that DMT acts as a **neuromodulator** rather than just an exogenous psychedelic
