# Music and Icaros in Ayahuasca Ceremonies

## Overview

Music, particularly icaros (sacred songs from Amazonian shamanic traditions), is considered essential to ayahuasca ceremonies in most traditional contexts. Research from Johns Hopkins and Imperial College London confirms that music-responsive brain regions show increased activation during psychedelic experiences, and that the quality of musical support predicts mystical experience intensity.

## The Role of Music in Ayahuasca Experiences

### Traditional Understanding

In Amazonian traditions, icaros are songs that "call the medicine" and guide the trajectory of the ceremony. Shamans learn icaros from the plants themselves during their own visionary experiences, and the songs are considered as much medicine as the ayahuasca brew.

**Functions of Icaros:**
- **Invocation**: Calling forth healing entities and energies
- **Direction**: Guiding the emotional/spiritual trajectory
- **Containment**: Creating a protective energetic boundary
- **Healing**: Direct medicinal properties in their own right
- **Connection**: Linking participant to traditional lineage

## Musical Elements in Ayahuasca Ceremonies

### Traditional Instruments

| Instrument | Role | Characteristics |
|------------|------|-----------------|
| **Maracas/Secos** | Rhythmic accompaniment | Shake patterns following breath and energy |
| **Shaker** | Pulse maintenance | Creates continuous rhythmic foundation |
| **Tambor** | Drumming during intense phases | ~60-80 BPM mimicking heartbeat |
| **Violão** | Harmonic support | Provides chord progressions |
| **Voice** | Primary vehicle | Human voice carries intention and spirit |

### Musical Phases

**Phase 1: Abertura (Opening)**
- Softer, welcoming icaros
- Establishes sacred space
- Prepares participants for inward journey
- Tempo: Slow, measured

**Phase 2: Guerra (War/Cleaning)**
- More intense icaros during purge
- Supports release and catharsis
- Drives out negative energies
- Rhythm: More driving, insistent

**Phase 3: Corrida (Running)**
- Fast, energetic songs during peak
- Accelerates the experience when appropriate
- Rhythmic complexity increases
- Often includes tobacco prayers (defumadores)

**Phase 4: Descida (Descent/Calming)**
- Slower songs as effects moderate
- Allows reintegration
- Grounds participants
- Tempo: Gradually slowing

**Phase 5: Fechamento (Closing)**
- Final songs of gratitude
- Seals the ceremony
- Blesses participants
- Often includes Santa Maria

## Categories of Icaros

### By Function

**Icaros de Cura (Healing Songs)**
- Address specific ailments
- Call healing energies
- Used during medicine administration

**Icaros de Protección (Protection Songs)**
- Create energetic boundaries
- Keep negative entities away
- Invoke protective spirits

**Icaros de Limpra (Cleansing Songs)**
- Support purging process
- Clear accumulated energies
- Release emotional baggage

**Icaros de Convocação (Invocation Songs)**
- Call specific entities or energies
- Summon plant spirits
- Attract particular helpers

**Icaros de Luz (Light Songs)**
- Call forth luminous beings
- Increase visionary intensity
- Deliver messages from light entities

### By Tradition

**Santo Daime Hymns (Hinários)**
- Hundreds of songs composed by Mestre Irineu
- Blend Christian and indigenous elements
- Include psychedelic and spiritual content
- Often called "concentrations" or "大人的 songs"

**UDV Songs**
- Unique repertoire specific to União do Vegetal
- Theological content reflecting Mestre Plinio's teachings
- Portuguese and indigenous language mix

**Indigenous Shipibo/Asháninka**
- Shipibo-Conibo tradition most documented
- Complex, haunting melodies
- Often learned directly from plant spirits
- Geometric patterns in visualizations

### The Shaman's Role

The traditional ayahuasquero (shaman) uses icaros as primary healing instruments:

1. **Diagnosis**: Perceiving what needs to be addressed through altered vision
2. **Selection**: Choosing appropriate icaros for the moment
3. **Timing**: Delivering songs at optimal moments
4. **Modulation**: Adjusting intensity to participant needs
5. **Integration**: Knowing when silence serves better

## Music in Non-Traditional Contexts

### Contemporary Integration

Many Western ayahuasca contexts blend traditional and contemporary approaches:

**Playlist Elements:**
- Traditional icaros recordings
- Ambient electronic music
- World music (didgeridoo, Tibetan bowls)
- Sacred music (Gregorian, Sufi)
- Silence as deliberate element

### Research Considerations

The Imperial College London psilocybin studies use a curated music playlist specifically designed for psychedelic therapy sessions. Key principles:

- Begin with quiet, unobtrusive music
- Build to emotionally impactful pieces at peak
- Return to quiet as session concludes
- Match music to expected phase of experience
- Allow flexibility for participant response

## Practical Applications

### For Ceremony Preparation

If facilitating or attending ceremonies:

1. **Research the tradition** - understand the musical lineage
2. **Prepare emotionally** - music can amplify whatever is present
3. **Stay open** - allow music to guide experience
4. **Don't fight** - surrender to the sonic landscape
5. **Journal** - note which songs affected you and how

### For Integration

After ceremonies:

1. Listen to recordings of ceremonial music
2. Notice what arises emotionally
3. Connect musical moments to insights
4. Find personal playlists that support integration
5. Consider learning to sing icaros yourself

## Health and Safety Considerations

### Contraindications

- Certain musical elements may trigger trauma in sensitive individuals
- Loud sudden sounds can cause distress
- Some participants prefer silence to music

### Cultural Considerations

- Not all icaros are meant for all people
- Some songs carry specific lineage restrictions
- Respect for traditional knowledge systems matters
- Avoid appropriating without understanding context
